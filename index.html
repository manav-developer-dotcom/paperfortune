The Studio

We work with sunlight as our primary medium, allowing the unpredictable nature of exposure to guide each piece toward its final form.

Sun as Printer

The cyanotype process was invented in 1842 by Sir John Herschel. We have adapted this photographic technique to textiles, creating wearable blueprints that carry the ghost of botanical forms.

Each morning, we coat fabric with iron salts in near-darkness. By afternoon, the sun has done its work. By evening, water reveals what light has written.

Botanical Collaboration

We collect specimens during specific seasons — ferns in spring, seed heads in autumn, eucalyptus year-round. Each plant leaves its signature differently depending on the day's UV intensity.

The arrangement is never fully controlled. Wind shifts a leaf. A cloud passes. These accidents become the most beautiful passages.

Imperfection as Value

In an age of mass production, we choose slowness. A single piece may take three days from coating to finished object. We embrace the brush marks, the uneven edges, the slight variations that prove human hands were involved.

No two pieces will ever be identical. Even if we tried, the sun would refuse to cooperate.

Understanding the Medium

Cyanotype & Care

A living process that continues to breathe after it leaves our studio

What is Cyanotype?

A photographic printing process that produces a cyan-blue print. The technique uses a mixture of iron compounds, which when exposed to UV light and washed in water, create the distinctive Prussian blue pigment. Invented in 1842, it was originally used to reproduce notes and diagrams — hence "blueprint."

Why Every Piece is Unique

Sun intensity, humidity, cloud cover, and the specific chemistry of each coating session all influence the final result. We cannot replicate conditions exactly, even if we wished to. This means your piece is truly one-of-a-kind, carrying the specific atmospheric conditions of its creation day.

The Beauty of Fading

Cyanotype is light-sensitive by nature. Prolonged exposure to strong direct sunlight will gradually soften the blue. We view this not as degradation, but as evolution — the textile developing a patina that reflects its life with you. Many collectors specifically seek out the softer, weathered tones that develop over years.

Restoring the Blue

If you wish to temporarily deepen the color, storing the piece in a dark drawer for several days will allow the blue to regenerate. This reversibility is part of cyanotype's magic — it is never truly finished, always in dialogue with its environment.

Preservation

Care Instructions

◦

Cold Wash

Hand wash or machine wash on cold gentle cycle. Hot water will accelerate fading.

◦

Gentle Detergent

Use pH-neutral soap. Avoid bleach, optical brighteners, and harsh chemicals entirely.

◦

Shade Dry

Dry flat in shade. Direct sun during drying will cause immediate color shift.

◦

Store Dark

When not in use, store away from direct sunlight to preserve the depth of blue.
